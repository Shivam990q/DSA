# 🔮 The Inventor Mindset

> *"To be an inventor is to live in the question 'why not?'"*

---

## I. THE TEN HABITS OF ALGORITHM INVENTORS

### 1. **Constant pondering**
Some problem is in the back of their mind 24/7. Walking, showering, sleeping. The mind chews. Insights come unbidden.

### 2. **Notebook discipline**
A physical notebook. Every idea, no matter how wild, gets written. Most are wrong. The few right ones change the field.

### 3. **Cross-pollination**
They read outside their field. Math, physics, biology, economics. Deepest algorithms steal from neighbors.

### 4. **Specific problem obsession**
A single open problem becomes a personal companion for years. Tarjan on dynamic graphs. Karger on min-cut.

### 5. **Implementation discipline**
They CODE their algorithms. Not just on paper. Bugs reveal flaws in proofs.

### 6. **Teaching pressure**
They teach informally — to peers, students. Teaching forces complete understanding.

### 7. **Counterexample seeking**
Their first instinct on a new conjecture: try to break it. Build, then attack.

### 8. **Generalization reflex**
A solution to one problem → "what's the most general problem this technique solves?"

### 9. **Simplicity worship**
Knuth: "Simple algorithms are beautiful, beautiful algorithms are simple." They strive for the cleanest expression.

### 10. **Patience with the unknown**
Comfort with not knowing. Comfort with months / years of partial progress. Most invent only 2-3 things in a career.

## II. THE INVENTOR'S DIET

### Daily
- 1 hard problem (open or hard, not "do solved")
- 1 paper page (deep read)
- 1 conversation with a peer

### Weekly
- 1 talk attended (online or in-person)
- 1 blog post or note written
- 1 implementation of something existing (to learn the form)

### Monthly
- 1 deep paper fully understood
- 1 conjecture made (your own)
- 1 attempt at a small original contribution

### Yearly
- 1 paper / blog / artifact published
- 1 specialty deepened
- 1 collaboration started

---

## III. WHERE INVENTION HAPPENS

### Edge of the literature
Read what's "almost done." Ask: "What's the next simple step?"

### Connections
Take a tool from field A; apply to field B. (Compressed sensing came from this.)

### Computational experiments
Run code on small cases. Notice patterns. Hypothesize.

### Failure analysis
A known algorithm fails on certain inputs. Why? Often the path to a new variant.

### Re-derivations
Re-derive a famous algorithm from scratch. Often you find a slight improvement.

## IV. WHAT INVENTING FEELS LIKE

> *"Most days, you stare at a problem and feel stupid.  
>  Some days, an idea comes — usually wrong.  
>  Rarely, an idea comes — usually right.  
>  When right comes, you feel less stupid for a few hours.  
>  Then you find a flaw and feel stupid again.  
>  This is the work."*

---

## V. THE TEN MOST COMMON DELUSIONS OF ALGORITHM INVENTORS

1. **"This is original."** — usually it's been done. Search hard before claiming.
2. **"This is fast."** — always benchmark.
3. **"This will scale."** — always test on big inputs.
4. **"My proof is rigorous."** — peer-review or it doesn't count.
5. **"My implementation is correct."** — stress-test, ALWAYS.
6. **"I should keep this secret."** — share early; feedback is gold.
7. **"It's too obvious to publish."** — many "obvious" results are publishable.
8. **"It's not novel enough."** — incremental progress is real progress.
9. **"I need a PhD."** — many famous inventors had no PhD or different background.
10. **"I'm not smart enough."** — every grandmaster started as a beginner.

## VII. RICHARD HAMMING'S RULES (from "You and Your Research")

For doing first-class work:

1. **Pick the right problem.** Not just any problem — the right one for you.
2. **Work hard.** Genius is overrated; sustained focus underrated.
3. **Tolerate ambiguity.** Open problems don't have neat statements.
4. **Show up.** Day after day. Even when uninspired.
5. **Communicate.** Half of doing research is making people understand it.
6. **Don't be the one who almost did it.** Finish.
7. **Sell your ideas.** Don't be shy about your work.

## VIII. THE GRANDMASTER'S PRAYER

> *"Let me see what others miss.  
>  Let me prove what I claim.  
>  Let me share what I find.  
>  Let me not be discouraged by the slowness of progress.  
>  Let me not be inflated by occasional success.  
>  Let me be a worthy steward of the algorithmic tradition."*
