# 🏁 Contest Training System

> *"You don't rise to the occasion. You sink to your preparation."*

---

## I. WHY CONTESTS?

Contests train what practice can't:
- **Speed under pressure**
- **Decision-making with incomplete info**
- **Time management across multiple problems**
- **Emotional regulation** (handling WA, TLE)
- **Pattern recognition at velocity**

You can be a great solver and a poor contestant. Contests fix that.

---

## II. THE CONTEST CALENDAR

### Weekly minimum
- 1 live contest ([Codeforces](https://codeforces.com) Div 2/3 or [AtCoder](https://atcoder.jp) ABC)
- 1 virtual contest (recreate a past round under timed conditions)

### Optimal
- 2-3 live contests
- 2 virtual contests
- Full upsolving of each

---

## III. THE PRE-CONTEST ROUTINE

```
24 hours before:
  - Light practice only (no heavy new concepts)
  - Sleep 8 hours

2 hours before:
  - Eat (avoid heavy carbs)
  - Hydrate

30 min before:
  - Warmup: solve 1 easy problem
  - Open template, editor, scratch paper
  - Bathroom break
  - 3 deep breaths
```

---

## IV. IN-CONTEST PROTOCOL

```
Min 0-5:    Read ALL problems. Sort by mental difficulty.
Min 5-X:    Solve easiest first. Bank quick points.
            For each problem:
              - Understand → brute complexity → optimize → code → test → submit
Switch rule: 30-40 min stuck → switch problems, return later
Last 10 min: Verify all submissions. NEVER submit untested code in panic.
```

---

## V. THE POST-CONTEST PROTOCOL (THE GOLD)

The contest itself is 30% of the value. **Upsolving is 70%.**

```
Immediately after (30 min):
  - Note which problems you solved / failed / didn't attempt
  - Record your emotional state and decision mistakes

Next day (2-3 hours):
  - Upsolve ALL unsolved problems WITHOUT editorial (give 30-60 min each)
  - Then read editorial for remaining
  - Re-implement from understanding (no copy-paste)
  - Note the pattern in your journal
```

## VII. HANDLING THE EMOTIONAL ROLLERCOASTER

### When you get WA
- Don't panic. WA is information.
- Re-read problem. Check edge cases. Stress test.
- Don't rage-resubmit; that wastes penalty time.

### When you're stuck
- Switch problems. Come back fresh.
- Re-read; you may have misunderstood.

### When you tank a contest
- 24 hours of zero practice.
- Then upsolve mercilessly.
- One bad contest ≠ your skill. It's variance.

### When you do great
- Enjoy it. Then upsolve the unsolved.
- Don't get complacent. Variance cuts both ways.

---

## VIII. THE VIRTUAL CONTEST DISCIPLINE

Pick a past contest you haven't seen. Set a strict timer. Treat it like live.

Benefits:
- Practice contest conditions anytime
- No rating risk → can experiment
- Immediate editorial access for upsolving

Recommended: 1 virtual per week, picking contests at your target difficulty.

---

## IX. THE CONTEST-TYPE PLAYBOOKS

### Codeforces Div 2 (2-2.5 hours, 5-6 problems)
- A, B = warmup (solve in 20 min total)
- C = the differentiator (spend deeply)
- D, E = climb if time

### AtCoder ABC (100 min, 7-8 problems A-G/Ex)
- A-D usually fast
- E-F are the rating movers
- Ex is for yellow+

### Long challenges (CodeChef, 1-10 days)
- Pace yourself
- Partial scoring → solve subtasks first
- Less time pressure, more depth

### ICPC-style (5 hours, team)
- See `../12-COMPETITIVE-PROGRAMMING-UNIVERSE/12-COMPENDIUM-CP-Wisdom.md`

---

## X. THE 100-CONTEST MILESTONE

After 100 rated contests (live + upsolved):
- You'll have solved ~400-500 contest problems
- Your contest temperament will be steel
- Your rating will reflect your true skill (variance averages out)
- You'll recognize patterns at velocity

Consistency over 100 contests > intensity over 10.
